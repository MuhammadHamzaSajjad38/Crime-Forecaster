🤖 Technology & Models

A deep dive into the machine learning algorithms powering our crime prediction engine.

Our Modeling Approach

To ensure the highest accuracy and robustness, we did not rely on a single algorithm. Instead, we explored multiple state-of-the-art machine learning techniques to understand the complex patterns in the Chicago crime dataset. We analyzed the performance of Random Forest, a Multi-Layer Perceptron (Neural Network), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks before creating a final, powerful ensemble model.

Random Forest

Random Forest is a powerful ensemble learning method that operates by constructing a multitude of decision trees at training time. For classification, the final decision is made by a majority vote of all the trees. It excels at handling complex, non-linear data and is highly resistant to overfitting.

Role in Project: Used as a strong baseline and a key component of our final voting ensemble due to its high accuracy.

Neural Network (MLP)

A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is a class of feedforward artificial neural network. It consists of at least three layers of nodes: an input layer, a hidden layer, and an output layer. MLPs are excellent at learning complex patterns and are capable of approximating any continuous function, making them ideal for classification tasks.

Role in Project: Provided a deep learning perspective and contributed a different "view" of the data to our ensemble model.

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)

LSTMs are a special kind of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) capable of learning long-term dependencies. They are specifically designed to avoid the long-term dependency problem by remembering information for long periods. This makes them highly effective for time-series data, such as crime patterns over time.

Role in Project: Explored for its potential to capture temporal sequences in crime data, though the final model prioritized location-based features.

The Final Ensemble Model

Our final, deployed model is a **Voting Classifier Ensemble**. This meta-estimator combines the predictions from our Random Forest, MLP, and K-Nearest Neighbors models. By using a 'hard' voting mechanism, it selects the crime type that is predicted most frequently by the individual classifiers. This approach leverages the diverse strengths of each algorithm to produce a more accurate and reliable final prediction than any single model could achieve on its own.
